Birth of a Universe — ECSK Bounce

You are looking at a simplified picture of a star collapsing into a black hole, then bouncing into a new expanding universe in Einstein-Cartan theory.

The tube-like surface shows how the size of that closed interior universe changes over time. Higher up, the universe is collapsing. At the narrow red ring, the collapse stops. Below that, the baby universe expands.

In this model, particle spin creates torsion — a repulsive effect that becomes stronger faster than ordinary gravity during extreme compression, so the collapse halts at a finite minimum size instead of crushing to a point (a singularity).

This drawing shows a single cut through the geometry so you can read it on screen. In the full picture, the collapsing star interior and the black hole are spherical, so the same bounce structure wraps all the way around in every direction.

More formally, the radius at each height is the scale factor a̅(τ̅), computed from:

  (da̅/dτ̅)² = 1/a̅² − β/a̅⁴ − 1

● Contraction — parent star interior collapsing
○ Event horizon — a̅ = a̅max = rS (Schwarzschild radius)
● Bounce hypersurface — torsion halts collapse at a̅min
● Expansion — baby universe is born
◉ Viewing axis — reference line showing the direction of view from inside the baby universe

The bounce surface is a thin 2D sheet, so you do not see particles flying toward or away from you in 3D. The main simulation only lights up the instant each particle crosses that surface from collapse into expansion.
